An Open Letter — March 2026

The Cardinals Fan
Hall of Fame

Three years documenting Busch Stadium III. A letter from Harry Caray on Anheuser-Busch letterhead. Postcards from the 1950s with the Cardinals playing Philadelphia, New York, Boston. A photo album my father gave me when I was six years old.

By Gregory Woodruff — MissouriConstruction.com — Published March 2026

"I really was just a web hosting guy who went where the customers were. I called the domain Ballpark Construction dot com. I showed up on Day 1. I bought hats, I brought donuts to the trailer. I had no idea what I was about to document for the next three years."

Day One

January 2004. The wrecking crews had been on site for days, but I wanted a specific photograph: the last remaining light standards at the south end of the old Busch Stadium, framing a direct sightline to the Gateway Arch. Standing at that exact location meant something. Because that precise spot — right there, by that light pole on the south end — is where home plate sits in the new stadium today.

I took that photograph. Looking northwest. The Arch in the background, the old steel in the foreground. 630 feet of stainless steel, the tallest structure in Missouri by law — no building in St. Louis may surpass it.

I did not fully appreciate at the time that I was standing at the only location in all of Major League Baseball where a fan can sit in the stands and look out past the outfield wall and see a National Monument. Jefferson National Expansion Memorial. The Gateway Arch. That view belongs to Busch Stadium III and nowhere else in the major leagues. I documented the construction of that view, from the first shovel of dirt to Opening Day 2006.

Why I Kept Coming Back

I was building a web hosting company. My target clients were union contractors — the men and women pouring concrete, running conduit, setting steel on sites like this every day across the St. Louis metro. Going to the construction site was just smart marketing. You go where the customers are. I went. I left donuts at the trailer. I handed out hats that said BallparkConstruction.com. I landed eight or ten accounts from those visits.

But I kept coming back when there was no marketing reason to. On cold January mornings. After a summer rainstorm when the light was magnificent. On weekends when the site was quiet and you could hear the steel hum. I brought a camera and I did what any Cardinals fan who loves this city would do: I paid attention.

One of the people I photographed in 2000 — while I was documenting the old Busch Stadium — was a young electrician working in his truck on some sign components at the southeast corner of the park. His name is Chuck Sword. He's a journeyman with IBEW Local 1 and works for a company called Worn Signs — the company responsible for all signage work at Busch Stadium, then and now. I photographed Chuck in 2000 without knowing his name. We met in 2017 and became close friends. Last night he came over and gave me a haircut. That's what this city does. It connects people across time.

The Letter from Harry

I have in my possession a letter from Harry Caray, written on official Anheuser-Busch letterhead. If you've been a Cardinals fan long enough to remember Harry behind that microphone — "Holy Cow!" — you know what that means. You know the weight that letterhead carries. The Budweiser connection. The voice of summer. I've been careful with it. I don't say much about it publicly. But it exists, and it is remarkable.

The Postcards

My father gave me his photo album when I was six or seven years old. I kept it. Through grade school, through high school, through four decades of moving and building and rebuilding. Most of the baseball cards that were in it are gone — I didn't know their value then, which is the universal story of every collector who came of age before grading companies existed.

But I still have the postcards. Wildwood. Rochester, Illinois. Period postmarks. Two-cent stamps. Players from the Cardinals, the Cubs, the Dodgers, the Yankees — six or seven teams represented on postcards from the early 1950s, when there were only sixteen teams in all of Major League Baseball. These aren't reproductions. They aren't laminated. They are original, postmarked, handled-by-someone-we'll-never-know cards from before the interstate highway system existed.

I have some of them scanned. I'll be sharing them here.

The Proposal

The St. Louis Cardinals should be the first Major League Baseball franchise in America to create a Fan Hall of Fame.

Not for players. Not for managers. Not for front-office executives. For fans. For the generations of families who poured their love, their time, their heirlooms, and their stories into this franchise — and who have no institution to receive them.

Here is how it would work:

  • 1. Fans donate collections on loan. The family retains ownership. The Cardinals serve as curator — providing museum-quality storage, cataloging, and professional assessment. The family's name goes on the exhibit, permanently.
  • 2. Two or three featured weekends per year — a three-game series weekend where the Fan Hall features ten rotating exhibits. Families are invited back. The docents are Cardinals historians. The community gathers around real artifacts.
  • 3. The Cardinals become the largest curated fan collection in the country. Baseball cards are a religion right now. The grading market has made cards more valuable than ever. The Cardinals have the most passionate fan base in the National League. The timing is right.
  • 4. When a fan passes away and the family decides to permanently place a collection with the Cardinals, the organization becomes the world's premier repository of Cardinals memorabilia — outside Cooperstown — with provenance, family story, and historical context attached to every item.

This is not unprecedented. Great art museums do this. Natural history museums do this. The Smithsonian does this. Baseball just hasn't done it for its fans, only for its players.

I'm not suggesting my collection is the most extraordinary the Cardinals would receive. I'm just the guy with three years of construction photos, a Harry Caray letter, and some 1950s postcards who happens to believe this idea is worth saying out loud. There are fans in St. Louis with Lou Brock's batting gloves, with scorecards from the 1944 All-St. Louis World Series, with photographs that have never been digitized. The Cardinals have the ability to be their permanent, credentialed home. And the first franchise to do it will be remembered for it.

The Gallery Is Live

The Busch Stadium III construction gallery is now available at MissouriConstruction.com. Three years. Hundreds of visits. Organized by construction phase and season. You will see the steel go up. You will see the Arch in the background. You will see the stadium that has housed eleven division titles and two World Series championships take shape from an empty block into something that belongs to this city.

And somewhere in those photos — in the background of a January morning shot, working on some sign components near the east facade — you might just spot a young electrician in a truck. His name is Chuck.
